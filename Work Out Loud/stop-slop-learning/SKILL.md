# Stop Slop: Learning Design Edition

A skill for identifying and removing AI writing patterns from instructional content — course scripts, learning objectives, facilitation guides, job aids, storyboards, and e-learning narration.

---

## The Problem

AI-generated instructional content has a fingerprint. It sounds like every other course. Learners feel talked at. Objectives read like legal disclaimers. Scenarios involve fictional characters named "Alex" who "navigate challenges." The writing signals effort without doing any.

This guide targets the specific slop patterns that infect learning design work.

---

## Core Rules

### 1. Cut filler phrases
Throat-clearing, emphasis crutches, instructional jargon — see `references/phrases.md`.

### 2. Break formulaic structures
Binary contrasts, rhetorical setups, narrator-from-a-distance voice — see `references/structures.md`.

### 3. Use active voice
Every sentence needs a subject doing something. "Learners will be able to..." hides who does what. Say "You will..." or restructure entirely.

### 4. Write objectives that mean something
An objective is a performance promise, not a vocabulary list. "Understand the importance of X" is not an objective. Name what the person does with X and when.

### 5. Put the learner in the room
Scenarios should feel like something that could happen to the reader next Tuesday. Disembodied case studies and fictional composites drain reality. Use second person. Name real stakes.

### 6. Trust the learner
Don't announce what you're about to teach. Don't summarize what you just taught unless the complexity demands it. Don't soften facts with encouragement. State things directly and let people think.

### 7. Vary the rhythm
Three-item lists. Bulleted everything. Topic sentence + two bullets + summary sentence. Learning content defaults to one template and stays there. Break it.

### 8. Kill scaffolding language
"In this module, we will explore..." "By the end of this lesson..." "Now that we've covered X, let's turn to Y..." These transitions exist for the writer's comfort, not the learner's understanding.

## Scoring (before finalizing any content)

Rate across five dimensions, 1–10 each:

| Dimension | What to check |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Directness** | Does every sentence say what it means without announcing it first? |
| **Rhythm** | Does the writing vary — sentence length, structure, paragraph weight? |
| **Trust** | Does it treat learners as capable adults who can handle direct information? |
| **Authenticity** | Does it sound like a person wrote it for a specific audience, or like a template? |
| **Density** | Does every sentence carry weight, or are some just filler between ideas? |

Score below 35/50: revise before using.

---

## Quick checks before finalizing

- [ ] Scan for "will be able to" — replace with specific, observable actions
- [ ] Scan for "explore," "unpack," "dive into," "journey" — cut or replace
- [ ] Scan for passive voice — name the actor
- [ ] Scan for "it's important to," "keep in mind," "remember that" — cut
- [ ] Scan for fictional composite personas ("Meet Alex, a mid-level manager...") — replace with direct second person
- [ ] Scan for objectives starting with "Understand" or "Appreciate" — rewrite as verbs that describe visible performance
- [ ] Scan for em dashes used for rhythm — remove
- [ ] Scan for three-part lists where two parts would do — trim
